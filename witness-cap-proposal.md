# Priced, Not Banned: A Per-Input Cap on Bitcoin's Witness Discount
**Author: BitByBitBullish. Concept by the author; developed and drafted with AI assistance (Claude, Anthropic). Status: idea paper for community review, not a BIP draft.**

## Abstract
Bitcoin's data war has two dug-in camps: filter advocates (Knots, BIP-110) want arbitrary data resisted at the rule level; neutrality advocates (Core) hold that valid fee-paying transactions must not be judged by content. Both defend something real. This paper proposes a two-part middle ground. First, cap the SegWit fee discount at the first 2,000 bytes of witness per input: every monetary transaction ever measured keeps its full discount, while bulk data beyond the cap remains valid but pays full weight. Second, pair the cap with a purpose-built discardable data lane (crediting Samson Mow's OP_RETURN2 concept and the taproot-annex lineage) so data needing only commitment migrates voluntarily to a channel nodes may drop after validation. No transaction becomes invalid. No UTXO can be frozen. No byte is judged by meaning. The cap is measured, not asserted: across 439,000+ inputs in two independent studies, the largest genuine monetary witness in an independent draw was 696 bytes; theory bounds the largest realistic construction (15-of-15 P2WSH) at 1,608 bytes. N=2,000 covers all of it with headroom.

## 1. The problem, stated fairly
The filter camp's case: the witness discount subsidizes non-monetary data at 25% of the price of base bytes; in our Era-C (2023+) measurement, 30.08% of all witness bytes are arbitrary data, rising to 72.08% in inscription-heavy blocks. Policy filters have proven bypassable (CVE-2023-50428, the `datacarriersize` bypass via `OP_FALSE OP_IF`, and CVE-2024-34149, unenforced Tapscript policy size limits — whose vulnerability classification is itself disputed; plus direct-to-miner submission), and the v30 harm-reduction theory has not been borne out — filter-side analyses document large-OP_RETURN usage surging after v30 (driven by a handful of pools) while witness embedding continued. Node operators bear bandwidth, storage, and legal-exposure costs for data that is not money.
The neutrality camp's case: content-judging rules are censorship regardless of intent, and blunt consensus caps catch real users — critique of BIP-110 by mempool.space developer mononaut documented roughly 32,000 historical spends with depth-100+ Taproot control blocks its limits would have hit, including rare fund-freezing edge cases, and a censoring-reorg incentive. Determined embedding cannot be prevented (demonstrated by embedding an entire proposal text with a decade-old, fully rule-compliant script). Mempool policy divergence harms fee estimation and mining decentralization.
Both cases are substantially correct on the evidence — which is why any proposal that picks a winner fails. The disagreement is not resolvable by filters or inaction. It is resolvable by pricing.

## 2. Design red lines (drawn from both camps)
1. No existing UTXO may become unspendable — never-confiscate is absolute.
2. No rule may read a byte's meaning — size and price only.
3. No legitimate monetary or contract use may pay more — multisig, Lightning, vaults, L2 constructions.
4. Node operators' worst case must improve, not worsen.
5. Activation only by broad consensus (≥90% signaling, generous timelines) — never minority brinkmanship.
6. Honesty about limits: determined embedding cannot be prevented; design for incentives, not prohibition.

## 3. Mechanism 1 — the per-input witness discount cap (N = 2,000)
Current consensus: transaction weight = 4×base_size + 1×witness_size; the 4 MWU block limit gives witness bytes a 75% discount, unlimited per input.
Proposed: per input, witness bytes up to 2,000 count 1 WU/byte exactly as today; witness bytes beyond 2,000 count 4 WU/byte.
Properties: (a) Soft fork — recomputed weights are greater than or equal to old weights for every transaction, so every block valid under the new rule is valid under the old rule; strictly tightening. No new transaction type, no script-language change. (b) Never invalid — any transaction of any size remains consensus-valid; it simply weighs more. No UTXO can be stranded; the worst case anywhere is a fee premium. This also eliminates the censoring-reorg attack class that ban-style proposals create, since no block containing data is ever invalid. (c) Single composite metric preserved — BIP-141 rejected dual limits because separate constraints wreck block templating and fee estimation; this design keeps one linear weight function. (d) One-time ecosystem cost, stated honestly — wallets and fee estimators update vsize math for the rare >2,000-byte-witness input; ordinary transactions are byte-identical before and after.

## 4. Why 2,000 — measured, not asserted
Two studies, scripts and per-input datasets published, seeded and reproducible (SEED=110). Study 1: stratified sample, 90 random blocks across three eras plus 10 inscription-heavy blocks, 387,174 inputs. Study 2: independent non-cluster draw, individual transactions from 751 distinct random blocks, 53,379 inputs (51,906 monetary).
Monetary witness sizes: median 104 bytes; the independent draw's maximum monetary input was 696 bytes (a contract-class spend), and the maximum core signature input 458 bytes (4-of-5 multisig). An apparent ~1,415-byte Lightning cluster in Study 1 traced to a single entity's one-block mass channel-close and did not reproduce in the independent draw; genuine Lightning spends measured 200–240 bytes.
Data witness sizes: p99 = 9,149 bytes; maxima above 200,000 bytes.
Theory, byte-exact: 15-of-15 P2WSH multisig, the largest realistically-used construction, is 1,593–1,608 bytes — fully covered at N=2,000 at zero cost. A 20-of-20 P2WSH (opcode maximum, empirically absent in 52k independent inputs) is 2,123–2,143 bytes and would pay ≈107 vB premium — never blocked. A depth-128 taproot control block (~4,129 bytes, standardness maximum, rare) pays a premium, never blocked.
Separation: every monetary input in the independent non-cluster draw sits ≥2.8× below the cap (max 696 B; core-signature max 458 B, ≈4.4× below); measured data traffic begins ~4.6× above it and runs 50–106× past it. The subsidy being removed is not hypothetical: 30.08% of Era-C witness bytes are data. Put plainly: across the 439,000+ inputs sampled, every input that would pay any premium under N=2,000 is data — not one genuine monetary spend in the independent non-cluster draw (51,906 monetary inputs) exceeds the cap. For scale, a typical embedded image runs 20,000–100,000+ bytes; the largest theoretical monetary construction (1,608 bytes) is less than a tenth of a small JPEG.

## 5. Honest cost analysis — including the sharding workaround
Naively embedded bulk data (one large-witness input) pays approximately 4× today's price beyond the cap. A determined embedder can shard data across many ≤2,000-byte-witness inputs, retaining the discount at the cost of per-input overhead and pre-created UTXOs; for a 100 KB payload the resulting premium versus today is only ≈1.2× (≈1.19× if the sharded inputs are unencumbered, ≈1.23× if each carries a signature) rather than 4×. The cap alone therefore raises bulk-data cost meaningfully but not decisively — which is precisely why Mechanism 2 exists.

## 6. Mechanism 2 — the discardable data lane (companion; credited)
Concept credit: Samson Mow's OP_RETURN2 sketch (a cheaper, fully discardable annex) and the taproot-annex lineage (reserved for future use since activation; TLV framing proposals exist). Design intent, presented at design level with spec questions open by intention: a consensus-committed, per-block-capped data area whose bytes are provably committed (a hash in the block preserves the timestamp/notary property forever), not required for UTXO validation, and therefore immediately droppable by any node. Priced at or below 1 WU/byte — cheaper than sharded witness, so rational data users migrate voluntarily and the sharding leak becomes economically irrelevant. Node operators end strictly better off than today: bulk data either pays full freight in permanent storage or lives in a lane they never store. Open engineering questions flagged honestly: commitment structure versus malleability and DoS, relay policy, interaction with the existing annex reservation. This paper proposes the destination, not the final wiring.

## 7. What this does not do — content honesty
Consensus rules see bytes, not meaning. No protocol rule — this one, BIP-110's, or any filter — can identify or exclude specific content: encryption defeats every classifier, and a content-judging rule would require an authority and break consensus determinism. What this design does instead: removes the subsidy, bounds worst-case node burden, and moves data toward a lane nodes need not store. Content accountability lives where it is real and legally enforceable — the indexer and rendering layer that reconstructs images from bytes. On-chain bytes are never viewable without such external software.

## 8. Activation
Conventional high-threshold deployment: ≥90% miner signaling with generous timelines, explicitly not a minority UASF — if the economic majority does not want this, it should not activate. Optional trial wrapper, borrowing BIP-110's one broadly defensible idea: activate with a fixed-duration sunset and pre-committed measurement criteria (data share of witness bytes, fee impact on monetary transactions, node-cost trend), with renewal requiring fresh consensus.

## 9. Objections, pre-answered
"It freezes funds" — nothing becomes invalid; premium, never prohibition. "It's censorship" — the rule cannot see content; a 50 KB image and a 50 KB script pay identically. "It hurts multisig, Lightning, or L2s" — measured: no real transaction pays more; theory: the largest realistic construction is fully covered; the exotic remainder pays ~100 vB and is never blocked (for scale, BIP-110's comparable limit is 256 bytes per witness data push — this proposal's 2,000 bytes per input is ~8× more generous, so constructions BIP-110 would break keep their full discount here). "Two limits wreck mining and fee estimation" — a single linear weight metric is retained, satisfying BIP-141's own rationale. "It won't stop determined spammers" — correct, and not claimed; it ends the subsidy and offers a cheaper lane nodes can drop; prohibition is impossible, pricing is not. "Miners lose revenue" — data may continue at full price or in the lane; volume falls while price-per-byte rises; the security-budget effect is ambiguous, not negative by construction. "Any consensus change is too risky" — the change is one weight formula with generous parameters, high-threshold activation, and an optional sunset; the status quo is also a choice, with measured costs.
